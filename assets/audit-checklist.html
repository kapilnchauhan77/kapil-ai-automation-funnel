The AI Automation Audit Checklist.

A 1-page self-audit to find the workflows in your business that AI can actually help — and the ones where it’s just another tool you don’t need. Walk through the five steps with one workflow in mind and you’ll know whether it’s worth automating before you spend a rupee.

§ 01 Map the workflow

Pick one painful workflow. Write down every step in order, including the silent ones (waiting on a person, switching tabs, copying data).

  • Trigger — what kicks it off, and how often?
  • Hand-offs — who touches it, in what order?
  • Tools — which apps, sheets, inboxes, files are involved?
  • Output — what does “done” actually look like?

§ 02 Spot the friction

Mark every step that has any of these markers. Each one is an automation candidate.

  • The same data is retyped into more than one place.
  • Someone reads a document, then enters numbers into a system.
  • Approvals or follow-ups happen through email or chat.
  • The step exists only to check that a previous step was done.
  • Mistakes here are caught a week later, not at the source.

§ 03 Score the opportunity

For each candidate, multiply two things. The high-leverage ones jump out instantly.

Frequency × Cost per run = annual leak
ex: 40 invoices/wk × 6 min × ₹500/hr
= ₹1,04,000/yr just to retype invoices.
Anything > ₹3 lakh/yr is worth automating today.

§ 04 Sanity-check feasibility

Before you build, confirm the boring stuff. AI projects fail here, not on the model.

  • The input is already digital (or trivial to digitise).
  • There is enough volume to justify a system, not a person.
  • You can describe “correct” clearly enough to test it.
  • You have someone who can sign off the output for the first month.
  • The cost of a wrong answer is recoverable, not catastrophic.

§ 05 Pick the first one to automate

If more than one workflow passes §03 + §04, choose by these tie-breakers, in order:

  • The one your highest-paid people spend the most time on.
  • The one whose errors most often reach customers or auditors.
  • The one whose data already flows through a system you own.
  • The one you can ship in 4 weeks, not 4 months.
